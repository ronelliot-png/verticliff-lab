Part 01

Write Your Premise Line

A premise line is the single sentence that contains your entire series. This is the single sentence that contains your entire series. If you can't compress your story to its core elements, you don't yet know what your story is. For vertical series, this sentence becomes the engine that drives every cliff, every episode, and every character decision across 25 episodes.

The VertiCliff™ Premise Method

When [a specific character] is [the push — what forces them into action], they must [pursue a concrete goal] — against [an opponent who wants the same thing].

01
The Push — What sets the story in motion

One specific event forces your protagonist into action. Not a backstory. Not a mood. A moment. The day everything changes. This is what launches Episode 1.

02
The Goal — What they are chasing

Your protagonist wants one concrete thing — something that can be won or lost by the final episode. The goal must be specific enough to fail at. "Justice" is not a goal. "Proving her brother didn't pull the trigger" is.

03
The Opponent — Who is fighting for the same thing

Your antagonist isn't just an obstacle — they want the same goal your protagonist wants, but for different reasons. That shared desire is what creates real, sustained conflict across 25 episodes.

04
The Change — Who your character must become

By the final episode your protagonist must be fundamentally different from who they were in Episode 1. What does this story force them to confront about themselves? That change is your series arc.

Classic Example — Tootsie

"When an actor can't get work, he disguises himself as a woman to land a role — only to fall in love with his co-star and be forced to become the man she deserves."

Push: can't get work. Goal: land the role. Opponent: his own dishonesty. Change: becomes a better man. One sentence — entire story.

  • One sentence only. If it takes two sentences, the premise isn't clear yet. Keep cutting.
  • The push must be specific. "Life gets hard" is not a kicker. "The night his business partner disappears with the money" is.
  • The opponent must want the same thing. Shared desire creates conflict that can sustain 25 episodes. A simple villain can't.
  • The change must be earned. Whatever your protagonist becomes by Episode 25 — it must be impossible without everything that happened before it.
  • Read it out loud. If it doesn't make someone ask "what happens next?" — rewrite it.
Part 02

Identify Your Genre

Your genre isn't just a category. In VertiCliff™ your genre determines which 8 beats get layered on top of your 22 universal beats. The wrong genre for your premise creates structural friction from Episode 1. Here are the three genres currently in the framework — and how to know which one fits your story.

Genre 01

Crime

A crime is committed, discovered, or covered up — and your protagonist is caught in the middle of the truth.

  • Your story involves deception, investigation, or justice
  • Someone knows something they shouldn't
  • The obstacle is another person protecting a secret
  • Every episode someone gets closer to — or further from — the truth
Genre 02

Thriller

Time is running out. The stakes are physical, immediate, and escalating — and survival is not guaranteed.

  • Your story has a ticking clock or point of no return
  • The danger is real and immediate — not emotional or relational
  • Your protagonist is being hunted, trapped, or manipulated
  • Every episode the threat gets closer and the options get fewer
Genre 03

Love

Two people want each other — but something keeps pulling them apart, and every episode tests whether love survives.

  • The core tension is emotional — not physical or criminal
  • The obstacle is internal, relational, or circumstantial
  • Your cliffs are emotional reversals — hope then doubt, closeness then distance
  • Every episode the relationship shifts — forward or backward

Not sure which genre fits? Ask yourself these questions.

What is the main source of tension in your story — a secret, a threat, or a feeling?
What does your audience feel at the end of each episode — suspicious, scared, or hopeful?
What does your protagonist want most — the truth, safety, or love?
If you had to describe your series in one word — is it dark, tense, or emotional?
